wss = []
k_range = range(1, 11)
for k in k_range:
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=k, n_init=25, random_state=42)
    kmeans.fit(clustering_input_scaled)
    wss.append(kmeans.inertia_)

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 4))
plt.plot(k_range, wss, marker="o")
plt.title("Elbow Method (WSS)")
plt.xlabel("Number of clusters (k)")
plt.ylabel("Within Cluster Sum of Squares")
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.xticks(k_range)
plt.show()

The WSS plot drops sharply up to k=4, then starts leveling off around k=5-6. That's the elbowit means adding more clusters after that point doesn't really help much. I'd recommend k=6 for KMeans. It strikes a good balance between getting useful detail for NANSE's geographic analysis and keeping things interpretable.

I named the clusters based on their standout features, looking at which metrics were highest or lowest in each group.

Cluster 0: Small, low profit, low promo - These are the smallest stores with the weakest profits and minimal promotion activity. Cluster 1: Medium, balanced - Pretty average across the board. Nothing stands out. Cluster 2: Large, profitable, high promo - The biggest cities with the best profits. Promotions work really well here. Cluster 3: Small, high velocity A - Small stores but their top products move fast. Cluster 4: Medium, promo heavy - Medium-sized stores that lean hard on promotions. Cluster 5: Low performers - Struggling across almost everythingsmall and unprofitable.

What NANSE should do: Focus on expanding in Cluster 2 citiesthese are your winners. The promotions work great there, so double down on that strategy in similar markets. Keep an eye on Cluster 0 too, since those smaller, struggling stores might have turnaround potential.
